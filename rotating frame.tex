A rotating frame of reference is a special case of a non-inertial reference
frame that is rotating relative to an inertial reference frame. An everyday
example of a rotating reference frame is the surface of the Earth. (This
article considers only frames rotating about a fixed axis. For more general
rotations, see Euler angles.)

All non-inertial reference frames exhibit fictitious forces. Rotating
reference frames are characterized by three fictitious forces

1 the centrifugal force

2 the Coriolis force

3 and, for non-uniformly rotating reference frames,

\ the Euler force.

Scientists living in a rotating box can measure the speed and direction of
their rotation by measuring these fictitious forces. For example, L\'{e}on
Foucault was able to show the Coriolis force that results from the Earth's
rotation using the Foucault pendulum. If the Earth were to rotate many times
faster, these fictitious forces could be felt by humans, as they are when on a
spinning carousel.

\section{Relating rotating frames to stationary frames}

The following is a derivation of the formulas for accelerations as well as
fictitious forces in a rotating frame. It begins with the relation between a
particle's coordinates in a rotating frame and its coordinates in an inertial
(stationary) frame. Then, by taking time derivatives, formulas are derived
that relate the velocity of the particle as seen in the two frames, and the
acceleration relative to each frame. Using these accelerations, the fictitious
forces are identified by comparing Newton's second law as formulated in the
two different frames.

\subsection{Relation between positions in the two frames}

To derive these fictitious forces, it's helpful to be able to convert between
the coordinates$\left(  x^{\prime},y^{\prime},z^{\prime}\right)  $ of the
rotating reference frame and the coordinates$\left(  x,y,z\right)  $ of an
inertial reference frame with the same origin. If the rotation is about the
axis with an angular velocity$\Omega$ and the two reference frames coincide at
time$t=0$ , the transformation from rotating coordinates to inertial
coordinates can be written%

\begin{align*}
x  &  =x^{\prime}\cos\left(  \Omega t\right)  -y^{\prime}\sin\left(  \Omega
t\right) \\
y  &  =x^{\prime}\sin\left(  \Omega t\right)  +y^{\prime}\cos\left(  \Omega
t\right)
\end{align*}


whereas the reverse transformation is%

\begin{align*}
x^{\prime}  &  =x\cos\left(  -\Omega t\right)  -y\sin\left(  -\Omega t\right)
\\
y^{\prime}  &  =x\sin\left(  -\Omega t\right)  -y\cos\left(  -\Omega t\right)
\end{align*}


This result can be obtained from a rotation matrix.

Introduce the unit vectors $\hat{i},\hat{j},\hat{k}$ representing standard
unit basis vectors in the rotating frame. The time-derivatives of these unit
vectors are found next. Suppose the frames are aligned at $t=0$ and the
$z$-axis is the axis of rotation. Then for a counterclockwise rotation through
angle $%
%TCIMACRO{\U{3a9} }%
%BeginExpansion
\Omega
%EndExpansion
t$:%

\[
\hat{i}\left(  t\right)  =\left(  \cos\Omega t,\sin\Omega t\right)
\]


where the $(x,y)$components are expressed in the stationary frame. Likewise,%

\[
\hat{j}\left(  t\right)  =\left(  -\sin\Omega t,\cos\Omega t\right)
\]


Thus the time derivative of these vectors, which rotate without changing
magnitude, is%

\begin{align*}
\frac{d}{dt}\hat{\imath}\left(  t\right)   &  =\Omega\left(  -\sin\Omega
t,\cos\Omega t\right)  =\Omega\hat{\jmath}\left(  t\right) \\
\frac{d}{dt}\hat{\jmath}\left(  t\right)   &  =\Omega\left(  -\cos\Omega
t,-\sin\Omega t\right)  =-\Omega\hat{\imath}\left(  t\right)
\end{align*}


This result is the same as found using a vector cross product with the
rotation vector$\Omega$ pointed along the $z$-axis of rotation$\Omega=\left(
0,0,\Omega\right)  $ , namely,%

\[
\frac{d}{dt}\hat{u}=\Omega\times\hat{u}%
\]


where is either $\hat{\imath}$ or$\hat{\jmath}$ .

\subsection{Time derivatives in the two frames}

Introduce the unit vectors representing standard unit basis vectors in the
rotating frame. As they rotate they will remain normalized. If we let them
rotate at the speed of about an axis then each unit vector of the rotating
coordinate system abides by the following equation:%

\[
\frac{d}{dt}\hat{u}=\Omega\times\hat{u}%
\]


Then if we have a vector function $f\left(  t\right)  =f_{x}\left(  t\right)
\hat{i}+f_{y}\left(  t\right)  \hat{j}+f_{z}\left(  t\right)  \hat{k}$,

and we want to examine its first dervative we have (using the product rule of
differentiation):%
\begin{align*}
\frac{d}{dt}f  &  =\frac{df_{x}}{dt}\hat{\imath}+\frac{d\hat{\imath}}{dt}%
f_{x}+\frac{df_{y}}{dt}\hat{\jmath}+\frac{d\hat{\jmath}}{dt}f_{y}+\frac
{df_{z}}{dt}\hat{k}+\frac{d\hat{k}}{dt}f_{z}\\
&  =\frac{df_{x}}{dt}\hat{\imath}+\frac{df_{y}}{dt}\hat{\jmath}+\frac{df_{z}%
}{dt}\hat{k}+\Omega\times\left(  f_{x}\hat{\imath}+f_{y}\hat{\jmath}+f_{z}%
\hat{k}\right) \\
&  =\left(  \frac{df}{dt}\right)  _{r}+\Omega\times f
\end{align*}


where $\left(  \frac{df}{dt}\right)  _{r}$is the rate of change of $f$ as
observed in the rotating coordinate system. As a shorthand the differentiation
is expressed as:%

\begin{align*}
\frac{d}{dt}f  &  =\left[  \left(  \frac{d}{dt}\right)  _{r}+\Omega
\times\right]  f\\
\frac{d}{dt}  &  =\left[  \left(  \frac{d}{dt}\right)  _{r}+\Omega
\times\right]
\end{align*}


This result is also known as the Transport Theorem in analytical dynamics, and
is also sometimes referred to as the Basic Kinematic Equation.

\section{Relation between velocities in the two frames}

A velocity of an object is the time-derivative of the object's position, or%

\[
V\overset{def}{=}\frac{dr}{dt}%
\]


The time derivative of a position $r\left(  t\right)  $in a rotating reference
frame has two components, one from the explicit time dependence due to motion
of the particle itself, and another from the frame's own rotation. Applying
the result of the previous subsection to the displacement , the velocities in
the two reference frames are related by the equation%

\[
V\overset{def}{=}\frac{dr}{dt}==\left[  \left(  \frac{d}{dt}\right)
_{r}+\Omega\times\right]  r
\]


where subscript $i$means the inertial frame of reference, and $r$ means the
rotating frame of reference.

\subsection{Relation between accelerations in the two frames}

Acceleration is the second time derivative of position, or the first time
derivative of velocity%

\begin{align*}
a_{i}\overset{def}{=}\left(  \frac{d^{2}r}{dt^{2}}\right)  _{i}  &  =\left[
\left(  \frac{d}{dt}\right)  _{r}+\Omega\times\right]  \left[  \left(
\frac{d}{dt}\right)  _{r}+\Omega\times\right]  r\\
&  =\left[  \left(  \frac{d^{2}}{dt^{2}}\right)  _{r}+\left(  \frac{d}%
{dt}\right)  _{r}\Omega\times+\Omega\times\left(  \frac{d}{dt}\right)
_{r}+\Omega\times\Omega\times\right]  r\\
&  =a_{r}+2\Omega\times v_{r}+\frac{d\Omega}{dt}\times r+\Omega\times\left(
\Omega\times r\right)
\end{align*}


where subscript $i$ means the inertial frame of reference. Carrying out the
differentiations and re-arranging some terms yields the acceleration in the
rotating reference frame%

\[
a_{r}=a_{i}-2\Omega\times\left(  \frac{dr}{dt}\right)  _{r}-\frac{d\Omega}%
{dt}\times r-\Omega\times\left(  \Omega\times r\right)
\]


where $a_{r}\overset{def}{=}\left(  \frac{d^{2}r}{dt^{2}}\right)  _{r}$is the
apparent acceleration in the rotating reference frame, the term $-\Omega
\times\left(  \Omega\times r\right)  $represents centrifugal acceleration, and
the term $-2\Omega\times\left(  \frac{dr}{dt}\right)  _{r}$is the coriolis effect.

\subsection{Newton's second law in the two frames}

When the expression for acceleration is multiplied by the mass of the
particle, the three extra terms on the right-hand side result in fictitious
forces in the rotating reference frame, that is, apparent forces that result
from being in a non-inertial reference frame, rather than from any physical
interaction between bodies.

Using Newton's second law of motion$F=ma$ , we obtain:

the Coriolis force%

\[
F_{Coriolis}=-2m\Omega\times v_{r}%
\]


the centrifugal force%

\[
F_{Coriolis}=-m\Omega\times\left(  \Omega\times r\right)
\]


and the Euler force%

\[
F_{Euler}=-m\frac{d\Omega}{dt}\times r
\]


where$m$ is the mass of the object being acted upon by these fictitious
forces. Notice that all three forces vanish when the frame is not rotating,
that is, when$\Omega=0$

For completeness, the inertial acceleration $a_{i}$due to impressed external
forces $F_{imp}$can be determined from the total physical force in the
inertial (non-rotating) frame (for example, force from physical interactions
such as electromagnetic forces) using Newton's second law in the inertial frame:%

\[
F_{imp}=ma_{i}%
\]


Newton's law in the rotating frame then becomes%

\[
F_{r}=F_{imp}+F_{centrif%
%TCIMACRO{\unit{gal}}%
%BeginExpansion
\operatorname{gal}%
%EndExpansion
}+F_{Coriolis}+F_{Euler}=ma_{r}%
\]


\emph{In other words, to handle the laws of motion in a rotating reference
frame:}

\emph{Treat the fictitious forces like real forces, and pretend you are in an
inertial frame.}

\emph{--- Louis N. Hand, Janet D. Finch Analytical Mechanics, p. 267}

\emph{Obviously, a rotating frame of reference is a case of a non-inertial
frame. Thus the particle in addition to the real force is acted upon by a
fictitious force...The particle will move according to Newton's second law of
motion if the total force acting on it is taken as the sum of the real and
fictitious forces.}

\emph{--- HS Hans \& SP Pui: Mechanics; p. 341}

\emph{This equation has exactly the form of Newton's second law, except that
in addition to F, the sum of all forces identified in the inertial frame,
there is an extra term on the right...This means we can continue to use
Newton's second law in the noninertial frame provided we agree that in the
noninertial frame we must add an extra force-like term, often called the
inertial force.}

\emph{--- John R. Taylor: Classical Mechanics; p. 328}
